Use this model to define the ICP from evidence rather than preference.

## Separate three artifacts

### 1. ICP / account fit
Who the company is.

Capture:
- industry or workflow vertical
- company size or complexity band
- geography if operationally relevant
- technical or process readiness
- buying trigger conditions
- disqualifiers

### 2. Buying committee map
Who gets involved in the decision.

Capture:
- economic buyer
- functional owner
- champion
- evaluator
- procurement/security reviewer if relevant

### 3. User/job context
What the end user is trying to get done.

Capture:
- primary job to be done
- pains in current workflow
- desired outcome
- switching friction

## Evidence hierarchy

Use sources in this order when available:

1. retained and expanded customers
2. recent closed-won analysis
3. churn and lost-deal analysis
4. customer interviews and call transcripts
5. product usage or retention patterns
6. market research and review language
7. enrichment and intent vendors

Lower-tier evidence can support a conclusion but should not override stronger direct evidence.

## Anti-ICP checklist

Document who is not a fit.

Include:
- segments with low retention
- small accounts with high support cost
- buyers who cannot sponsor the workflow change
- use cases where proof is weak
- teams lacking required data/process maturity

## Fit vs intent

Keep them separate.

- **Fit** = how well the account matches the product and workflow
- **Intent** = how likely the account is to act now

A strong fit account with low intent belongs in nurture, not immediate activation.
A weak fit account with high intent may signal ICP drift, but should not automatically become the new target.
