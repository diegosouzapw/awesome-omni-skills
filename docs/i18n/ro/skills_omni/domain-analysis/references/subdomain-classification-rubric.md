Classify candidate subdomains by business differentiation, not code volume.

## Core Domain

Choose **Core** when most of the following are true:

- it creates competitive differentiation
- the business wins or loses based on how well this capability works
- the rules are hard to standardize or buy
- domain experts are needed frequently
- the capability changes because strategy changes

## Supporting Subdomain

Choose **Supporting** when most of the following are true:

- it is necessary for the business to operate
- it contains business-specific rules
- it supports the Core Domain rather than being the main differentiator
- it may still be complex, but not strategically unique

## Generic Subdomain

Choose **Generic** when most of the following are true:

- it is common across many businesses
- standard products or common patterns exist
- the business does not compete primarily on this capability
- the implementation can be standardized with limited strategic loss

## Forced Questions

Answer these for every candidate subdomain:

1. What business capability does this provide?
2. Why is it Core, Supporting, or Generic?
3. What evidence supports that classification?
4. Could the capability reasonably be bought, standardized, or outsourced?
5. What would make this classification wrong?

## Common Misclassifications

### "It is critical, so it must be Core"
Not enough. Operational importance does not equal strategic differentiation.

### "It has the most code, so it must be Core"
Not enough. Code size often reflects historical accumulation, not business value.

### "It is hard to build, so it must be Core"
Not enough. Difficulty can come from integration or legacy complexity.

## Example Reminders

- authentication is often important but Generic or Supporting
- notifications are often Supporting or Generic
- billing may be Core in one company and Supporting in another
- recommendation logic may be Core where personalization is the competitive edge
